Normal time

This is how you are used to measuring time. There are 24 hours in a day, 60 minutes in an hour, and 60 seconds in a minute. You probably learned this in pre-school.

These weird numbers make no real sense and are a frequent nuisance. For example, try figuring out how many seconds are in three and a half hours. Or try converting 72 km/h to m/s. Or figure out at what time 20% of the day have passed. These calculations are unnecessarily confusing, aren't they?

Decimal time info_outlined

As opposed to the standard time measuring system, this system divides units into multiples of 10. The day is divided into 10 hours, one hour is divided into 100 minutes and one minute is divided into 100 seconds.

Since we represent numbers in the decimal system, decimal time allows us to convert between units by simply moving the decimal point:

  • 3.5 hours to seconds? Multiply by 100*100 (or move the decimal point four digits to the right): 3500 seconds.
  • 72 km/h to m/s? 1000 meters in a kilometer, 100 seconds in an hour, so we multiply by 1000/100=10 (or move the decimal point 3-2=1 digit to the right): 720 m/s.
  • 20% of the day? There are 10 hours in a day, so that would be exactly 2:00 o'clock. This also means an added bonus of easily converting something like 0.1415 days into hours: you just have to add the correct colons after one, three, and five digits (and add zeroes, if necessary)! So, 0.1415 days would be 1 hour, 41 minutes and 50 seconds
Decimal time units are emphasized; normal time units are not.
