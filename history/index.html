France1794 - During the French Revolution

Several different efforts to switch to decimal time were made in France throughout history. The most notable of which, however, was during the French Revolution. As part of a larger attempt at decimalisation (including switching to the metric system), France adopted decimal time on 22 September 1794. Its use was mandatory until 7 April 1795.

During that time, clocks were produced with both decimal and standard time, allowing for an easier conversion period. However, decimal time never caught on. In addition to decimalizing time, France also started dividing months into three décades of 10 days each. This change, however wasn't abolished until 1805.

China1st millennium BC

Chinese use of decimal time started by the 1st millennium BC. It was used alongside the standard time system and slightly differed from the system that was later adopted in France and that is explained on this site. Instead of dividing the day into 10 units of time, the Chinese divided it into 100 units called ke. However, this was changed from time to time. For example, from 5 to 3 BC, days were divided into 120 .
